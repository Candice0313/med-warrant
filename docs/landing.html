When AI judges medical AI, what does a verdict actually mean?

LLM-as-Judge is becoming the standard for evaluating medical answers at scale. This system asks: when the judge says it's 88% confident an answer is safe — but has no evidence behind it — should that verdict be trusted?

Before you open the demo
1

Verdict

The judge's assessment of the candidate answer on a given dimension — e.g. PASS, FAIL, PARTIAL, or NEEDS REVIEW.

2

Judge confidence

How certain the judge model is about that verdict. It is not a quality score for the candidate answer.

3

Evidence tier & displayed confidence

Evidence tier is the strength of external evidence supporting the verdict. Displayed confidence is judge raw confidence clamped down by the evidence ceiling — and, for some dimensions, an additional individual-scope cap.

4

Claim scope (individual vs population)

Each dimension is tagged population or individual. EBM tiers grade group-level evidence; claims about applying something to this specific patient are capped at 60%, regardless of tier. Other dimensions in the same case are unaffected.

Literature

What the research actually says

These are the papers and findings that shaped the design decisions in this system.

LLM overconfidence in medicine
The Illusion of Readiness arxiv 2509.18234 · Microsoft Research, Eric Horvitz, Eric Topol et al.

Six stress tests across six flagship models (GPT-5, Gemini 2.5 Pro, o3, o4-mini, GPT-4o, DeepSeek-VL2). The most striking result: swap the image that supports the wrong answer while keeping the text identical — GPT-5 drops from 83.3% to 51.7% (−31.6pp). The model was matching image-answer pairs from training, not reasoning about the image. Separately, CoT prompting decreased accuracy on several benchmarks, with models constructing confident-sounding chains of reasoning for hallucinated conclusions.

→ In this demo: Direct basis for evidence capping. If a model can be 83% confident on something it's essentially guessing, self-reported confidence cannot be the output signal. The complexity metadata tags (reasoning × visual dependency) come from this paper's clinician rubric framing.
LLM overconfidence on mARC-QA Scientific Reports, Nov 2025

Models including o1, Gemini, Claude, and DeepSeek perform significantly below physicians on clinical reasoning tasks, while expressing high confidence. Uncertainty analysis confirms systematic overconfidence despite limited accuracy.

→ In this demo: Empirical justification for the core rule: model confidence must be capped by external evidence, not taken at face value.
Evaluation frameworks
HealthBench OpenAI, May 2025 · 262 physicians, 60 countries, 48,562 rubric criteria

Evaluates along five axes: accuracy, completeness, context-sensitivity, communication quality, and instruction-following. Each criterion is written by a physician and weighted separately. No single aggregate score is reported as the headline output.

→ In this demo: Proof of concept that dimension-level evaluation at scale is viable. The dimension structure here (dosing accuracy, drug-interaction safety, etc.) is a direct analogue.
Known failure modes of LLM judges
Completeness detection is near-random

LLM judges cannot reliably identify incomplete medical answers — discrimination is near chance (~50%). Even when agreement is high, LLM judges and clinicians disagree fundamentally on why an answer is incomplete. False positives come mainly from over-flagging clinically unnecessary gaps. "Aggregate agreement masks deep disagreement in rationale."

→ In this demo: The "Completeness of warnings" dimension is deliberately shown at low confidence with a human-review flag — acknowledging this known blind spot rather than pretending the judge handles it reliably.
Demographic bias: detection fails entirely

Across ~3,000 responses, all tested LLMs failed to identify any degree of demographic bias. LLMs not only generate biased outputs — they cannot detect bias when it is presented to them.

→ In this demo: This system makes no attempt to evaluate fairness or demographic equity. That's a deliberate scope boundary, not an oversight.
Agent-as-judge and grounding
Agent-as-a-Judge ICML 2025, arxiv 2410.10934

Replaces a single-pass LLM judge with planning, tool-augmented verification, and multi-agent collaboration. Key difference: evaluates the process, not just the output — decomposes tasks into sub-tasks and verifies each step. The gain in human agreement comes from grounded verification, not bigger models.

→ In this demo: The evidence grounding step — requiring each verdict to cite a retrieved source — is this system's implementation of that idea. The inspectable snippets make the verification chain visible to the human reviewer.
Field consensus: LLM judge as assistant, not authority "Artificial Authority" · Bioengineering, Jan 2026

Across the literature, the consistent position is that LLM judges should assist human reviewers, not replace them. Safe deployment depends on strict human oversight and governance structures, especially for safety-critical verdicts.

→ In this demo: The Review Queue exists because of this. Escalated verdicts don't just get flagged — they go to a human who can Approve or Override with a note. The system is designed to surface decisions, not make them.
Limitations
J-HealthBench localization findings Hisada et al., arxiv 2509.17444, Sep 2025

~17% of benchmark items or rubric criteria required modification due to local clinical guidelines, healthcare system differences, or social norms (insurance schemes, drug legality, hotline numbers, etc.). A "globally valid judge" is itself questionable.

→ In this demo: Reason for not claiming the system generalizes. The evidence library and rubrics are synthetic and English-language; deployment in any real context requires local clinical validation.

LLMs are confidently uncertain

Language models report confidence scores, but those scores come from statistical patterns in training data — not from evidence retrieved for this specific question. A model saying "I'm 88% sure" about a drug interaction is making a very different claim than a systematic review that studied 10,000 patients.

In most LLM evaluation pipelines, the model's self-reported confidence is treated as a reliable signal. I think that's a mistake, especially in high-stakes domains like medicine. Confidence without a cited basis is just noise dressed up as a number.

The question I wanted to answer wasn't "is the AI right or wrong?" It was: "what is this confidence actually grounded in, and does the strength of that grounding match how confident the model sounds?"

Evidence sets a ceiling. It doesn't inflate.

The core rule in this system is simple: a verdict's displayed confidence is the model's confidence clamped down by the strength of evidence it grounded to. Evidence can only lower the number. It cannot raise it.

If the model says 88%, but the best evidence it found was an expert opinion (T5 ceiling: 60%), the displayed confidence is 60% — not 88%.

There's a separate cap for individual-scope dimensions — verdicts about whether the answer correctly applies to this specific patient, not just whether the general medical principle is sound. EBM evidence grades population-level patterns; it does not guarantee individual applicability. Those dimensions are capped at 60% even when the retrieved evidence is T1:

In the pediatric fever demo, Weight-based dosing is tagged individual and grounds to a systematic review (T1, ceiling 95%). The judge is 90% confident the answer fails. Displayed confidence is still 60% — not because the evidence is weak, but because the claim scope is individual. The Max daily dose dimension in the same case is population and can display 85% on the same T1 evidence.

There's a third rule for safety-critical claims specifically: if a verdict involves a safety recommendation and the only "evidence" is the model's own prior — no retrieved literature at all — the system flags it as UNVERIFIED SAFETY and routes it to a human reviewer. No exceptions. The model can sound maximally confident; it doesn't matter.

These three rules are the entire thesis. Everything else in the system is infrastructure to support them.

No aggregate score. On purpose.

The most natural thing to do in an evaluation system is to average the dimension scores into a single number. I didn't do that, and I think it would have broken the whole point.

If a case has three trustworthy verdicts and one safety escalation, an average might read "82% — looks fine." But that safety escalation is exactly the thing a reviewer needs to see. Averaging collapses the signal you actually care about.

The suppressed average is shown in the UI — struck through — as an explicit reminder that it exists but shouldn't be the headline.

The same logic applies to the radar chart in the Profile tab. It shows each dimension separately. There's no "overall shape" to optimize for. The amber dot on a low-confidence axis is the point, not noise to average away.

Explore Mode: evidence as a variable

The most interesting part of this system isn't the evaluation itself — it's the Explore Mode. You can change the evidence tier on any verdict and watch the confidence and escalation status update in real time, with no API calls.

The demo case I show is this: the drug-interaction safety verdict for warfarin + ibuprofen is grounded to a systematic review. It shows 88%, no escalation — correctly trusted. Now change the tier to "model prior." The row flips red. Confidence drops to 40%. UNVERIFIED SAFETY appears.

That single interaction communicates the thesis better than any explanation could. The number isn't a property of the model's answer. It's a function of the evidence behind it.

Six levels, six ceilings

The confidence ceilings are based on the Evidence-Based Medicine (EBM) evidence hierarchy — a framework developed in the 1990s to help clinicians weigh the reliability of different types of medical research. The core idea is simple: not all evidence is created equal. A single expert's opinion is less reliable than a randomized trial; a randomized trial is less reliable than a pooled meta-analysis of dozens of them. EBM formalizes this intuition into a ranked ladder.

The EBM hierarchy, from strongest to weakest
T1
Systematic reviews & meta-analyses Pool results from many studies, reducing the noise of any single trial
T2
Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) Randomly assign patients to treatment vs. control — the gold standard for causation
T3
Cohort & observational studies Follow patients over time without randomization — can show association, harder to show causation
T4
Clinical guidelines & consensus Expert panels synthesize the literature into recommendations — authoritative but one step removed from primary data
T5
Expert opinion & case reports Individual clinical experience or isolated cases — valuable but highly susceptible to bias
T6
Model prior No retrieved literature — the model is drawing entirely on patterns from training data, with no external anchor

The confidence ceilings in this system map directly onto this hierarchy. The numbers are deliberately conservative: a clinical guideline caps at 80%, not 95%, because guidelines encode consensus rather than direct trial evidence. Model prior caps at 40% — enough to surface a result, not enough to trust it.

Tier Evidence Type Confidence Ceiling
T1 Systematic review / meta-analysis  95%
T2 Randomized controlled trial (RCT)  90%
T4 Clinical guideline / consensus  80%
T3 Cohort / observational study  75%
T5 Expert opinion / case report  60%
T6 Model prior — no retrieved evidence  40%  → safety claims escalate

Individual scope: a second ceiling, per dimension

The tier table above applies to every dimension. But some dimensions ask a harder question: not "is this medically true in general?" but "did this answer get it right for this patient?" Those are tagged claim_scope: individual in the case definition. Examples include weight-based dosing for a specific child, monitoring advice for a patient on warfarin, or a sick-day check for someone on metformin.

For those dimensions only, displayed confidence is also capped at 60%, regardless of evidence tier.

Rules enforced by tests, not convention

90 pytest cases lock in the invariants. Breaking any of these rules breaks the build.

1

Displayed confidence ≤ evidence ceiling

Always. No exceptions for high-performing models or "obvious" cases.

2

Evidence never inflates confidence

A weak verdict grounded to a strong source cannot exceed the model's original confidence. Evidence only caps, never boosts.

3

Safety + model prior = automatic escalation

A safety-critical claim with no retrieved literature is flagged as DANGER and routed to human review, regardless of stated confidence.

4

Individual-scope claims get an extra cap

EBM evidence grades population-level patterns. Claims about applying something to a specific patient are capped at 60%, regardless of evidence tier.

5

No single aggregate score, ever

The suppressed average is shown struck-through in the UI. It exists, but it's not the output. Dimension-level variance is the signal.

Automation handles the easy cases. Humans handle the rest.

The system doesn't send everything to a human reviewer. That would be expensive and defeat the point of automation. But it also doesn't trust the model unconditionally — that would be dangerous. The design sits in between: two deterministic rules decide when a verdict needs a human, and everything else clears automatically.

Escalation Rule 1 — DANGER
Safety claim + no evidence

If a verdict is marked safety_critical and the best evidence found is T6 (model prior), the verdict is flagged as UNVERIFIED SAFETY and blocked from auto-approval.

Rationale: a safety recommendation with no external backing is exactly the failure mode that causes patient harm. The model's confidence is irrelevant.

Escalation Rule 2 — WARNING
Displayed confidence below 50%

If the capped confidence is below 0.50 — whether because the evidence is weak or the model itself was uncertain — the verdict is flagged as LOW CONFIDENCE and queued for review.

Rationale: below 50%, the verdict is essentially a coin flip. Passing it to the user without review is misleading.

Why two rules, not a model

The escalation rules are deterministic — not a trained classifier, not a probability threshold tuned on a dataset. This is intentional for two reasons.

Auditability. A reviewer can always see exactly why something was escalated: unverified_safety_claim or low_confidence. There's no black-box score to argue with.
Asymmetric cost. The cost of a missed safety escalation (patient harm) is orders of magnitude higher than the cost of an unnecessary review (a few minutes of a clinician's time). The rules are calibrated to err on the side of escalation.
Review Queue flow what happens after a verdict escalates
1
Verdict escalates during evaluation

Either rule fires → verdict tagged with reason + severity → saved to queue. The nav badge increments. The scorecard shows the escalation pill inline.

2
Reviewer opens the queue

Each card shows the case, dimension, escalation reason, model reasoning, and the inspectable evidence snippets that grounded (or failed to ground) the verdict.

3
Approve or Override

Approve — the model's verdict is confirmed. Card leaves the queue.
Override — reviewer adds a note explaining the correction. Both the note and the override reason are stored alongside the original verdict.
